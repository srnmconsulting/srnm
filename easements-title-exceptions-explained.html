Easements and Title Exceptions Explained

Easements and title exceptions are often listed in a title report long before anyone understands what they mean on the ground. Some can be mapped clearly. Others require deeper record work. Some affect use. Others are informational only.

A preliminary title report is not a site plan. It is a title document. It tells you what matters the title company intends to except from coverage, and it usually points to the recorded documents that create or describe those matters. For that reason, a title report is often the beginning of survey review, not the end of it.

What an easement is

An easement is a right associated with land that allows a use, access, or burden affecting property. It may benefit a utility, a neighboring parcel, a public agency, or a private party. Common examples include access easements, utility easements, sewer easements, slope easements, drainage rights, and rights of way.

Some easements are exclusive. Some are not. Some are tied to a defined strip of land. Some are blanket in nature and do not identify a precise location. The language of the recorded document matters.

What a title exception means

A title exception is not always the same as a mapped encumbrance. In many cases, the title company is listing a recorded document that may affect the parcel. The surveyor then reviews the underlying document to determine whether it can be located, how it should be shown, and what notes may be needed.

That review may show one of several outcomes. The exception may be clearly plottable. It may be blanket over the parcel. It may affect only an adjoining parcel. It may require additional record research. Or it may turn out to be too vague to locate with confidence from the available documents alone.

What can usually be mapped

Easements described by record map references, lot lines, centerlines, widths, bearings and distances, or monumented boundaries can often be plotted with reasonable confidence. Utility easements on subdivision maps are common examples. So are rights of way described as strips of a stated width lying along or adjacent to a known line.

The key question is whether the recorded description defines only one place on the ground. If it does, the matter is usually locatable. If it does not, the surveyor may still note it, but should be careful about presenting it as though its location were established when the record does not support that.

What often requires caution

Blanket easements, older utility rights with vague descriptions, agreements referring to facilities without tied exhibits, or title items that depend on missing attachments often require caution. A common problem is an exception that refers to a prior deed without enough information to determine whether the described rights affect the subject parcel, the adjoining parcel, or an off-site area.

Another common issue is when a title item is clear in the record, but there is no obvious physical evidence of it in the field. The absence of visible features does not make the right disappear. Conversely, visible use on the ground does not always mean a recorded easement exists.

Why the underlying document matters

Schedule B shorthand is rarely enough. The actual recorded document may contain exhibits, exceptions, reservations, conditions, prior references, or limitations that change the meaning of the title note. A one-line exception in the report may turn into several pages of rights, restrictions, and mapping implications once the underlying document is reviewed.

How survey review helps

Survey review can help sort title items into practical categories: plottable, approximately plottable, blanket, unclear, or likely outside the site. That helps a client decide what needs more attention. For attorneys and title teams, it can improve understanding of the record. For architects and engineers, it can help show where design constraints may exist. For owners and buyers, it can clarify whether a title exception appears to affect use, access, development area, or building location.

What survey review does not replace

A survey review of title exceptions does not replace legal advice. The surveyor’s role is to interpret, locate, and map survey-related record matters to the extent they can be supported. Questions about enforceability, priority between competing rights, or transactional risk may still require attorney review.

A practical way to approach title exceptions

Start with the current title report. Gather the recorded documents called out in Schedule B. Separate the matters that clearly affect the parcel from those that require more analysis. Then compare the record to the site and to any prior survey work. That process usually produces a much clearer picture than reading the title report by itself.

Helpful inputs for review

  • Preliminary title report
  • Schedule B documents
  • Prior survey exhibits
  • Current site plan
  • Access or utility questions from the project team

The better the underlying record set, the better the mapping and notes can be.

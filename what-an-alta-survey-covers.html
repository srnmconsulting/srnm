What an ALTA Survey Covers

An ALTA survey is not just a more expensive boundary survey. It is a title-oriented land survey prepared to a national standard for transactions where the parties need a clearer picture of boundary, improvements, access, easements, and other conditions that can affect insurability, financing, or commercial due diligence.

In commercial work, the request for an ALTA/NSPS Land Title Survey usually comes from the title, lender, buyer, or deal team rather than from a general desire to “just mark the lines.” The reason is practical. Those parties want a survey that ties the boundary work to title documents and shows what can be observed on the ground in a form they can review and rely on.

What “ALTA” means in practice

ALTA stands for the American Land Title Association, and the survey is prepared under the jointly issued ALTA/NSPS minimum standard detail requirements. The purpose is tied to title insurance. The standards are written for situations where the parties want survey matters investigated and shown so the title insurer, insured, lender, and client have a common understanding of the land being insured.

That is why the survey is both a field and record exercise. It is not limited to locating boundary monuments and drafting a plat. It also depends on title evidence, easement review, and specific reporting requirements.

It still includes boundary work

An ALTA survey does not replace boundary principles. It depends on them. The standards expressly require that the boundary lines and corners of the property be established or retraced in accordance with appropriate boundary law principles governed by the facts and evidence found during research and fieldwork.

That matters because some people hear “title survey” and assume the product is mostly paperwork. It is not. The title side is important, but the survey still stands on boundary evidence, monumentation, record research, and field judgment.

What records are typically part of the job

A proper ALTA survey usually begins with the current record description of the property, the title commitment or equivalent title evidence, and the recorded easements or servitudes affecting the site. Adjoiner information also matters, especially when the tract is not simply a lot in a platted subdivision.

This is one reason ALTA work often takes longer than a simpler survey request. The standards assume that the surveyor is working from a real document set, not just a street address and an assessor’s parcel number.

What the fieldwork usually covers

The field side of an ALTA survey is broad. It generally includes monuments, evidence of possession, buildings, access conditions, rights of way, visible easement evidence, certain utility evidence, water features where relevant, and improvements near the boundaries. The point is to show conditions that may affect title, use, access, or interpretation of the land records.

In practical terms, that often means the survey will show more than a typical owner expects at first: buildings with offsets, fences and walls near the lines, apparent encroachments, visible access points, utility poles or utility markings, rights of way, and recorded easements that can actually be plotted.

What the final plat usually includes

The final ALTA plat or map is expected to show the surveyed boundary, the record description or reference to it, boundary dimensions, basis of bearings, adjoining relationships where ascertainable, plotted easements, buildings, access information, and notes explaining relevant conditions or limits in the work.

It also typically includes a summary of easements and other survey-related title matters. Some can be plotted clearly. Some are blanket in nature. Some cannot be located from the record alone. A careful ALTA deliverable distinguishes between those categories rather than forcing certainty where the record does not support it.

Table A is where the scope often expands

One of the most misunderstood parts of an ALTA survey is Table A. Table A is the list of optional items that the client may request in addition to the base standard. These can materially change the scope, price, and timeline.

Depending on what is selected, the survey may also include items such as address confirmation, flood zone plotting, gross land area, vertical relief, zoning report information supplied by others, exterior building dimensions, parking counts, utility information from plans or private locates, adjoining owner names, distance to the nearest intersecting street, offsite easements, or other negotiated items.

This is why an ALTA proposal should not be treated as interchangeable from one project to the next. Two sites may both be called “ALTA surveys” while the actual scope differs substantially because of Table A selections, title complexity, site conditions, or offsite issues.

It is closely tied to title review

In actual practice, ALTA work sits close to title review. The surveyor studies the title commitment, reviews the listed exceptions and recorded easements, and determines what can be shown on the map, what is visible in the field, and what needs a note because the record is incomplete, blanket, or not locatable.

This is one of the reasons commercial clients, attorneys, and title officers value the work. The map is not just showing measurements. It is helping connect title items to the ground.

What it does not do

An ALTA survey is detailed, but it is not unlimited. It does not guarantee that every underground feature is known. It does not replace legal interpretation by counsel. It does not by itself cure title defects. It does not remove the need to read the title commitment, deed, lease, or easement documents carefully.

It is also important not to confuse the survey with a full engineering design base unless that extra work is specifically requested. Topography, utility research, zoning analysis, and other design-related items may be added, but they are not automatically included just because the word ALTA appears in the request.

Why commercial parties ask for it

In commercial transactions, people are often trying to answer questions before money is committed or coverage is issued. Is the building over the line. Does an access route actually exist on the ground. Is a recorded easement visible and where does it appear to lie. Does a neighboring improvement encroach. Is there evidence of use that is not well reflected by the record. An ALTA survey helps put those questions into a reviewable map.

For that reason, the audience is usually broader than the person paying the invoice. The final survey may be reviewed by the buyer, lender, title company, attorney, architect, civil engineer, and internal due diligence staff.

Why the certification matters

ALTA surveys include a specific certification format required by the standards. That certification identifies the parties to whom the survey is certified and confirms that the work was performed in accordance with the applicable ALTA/NSPS standard and selected Table A items.

This is one of the key differences between an ALTA survey and a more informal request for boundary information. The certification language is part of the deliverable, and the exact wording matters.

California work still has to satisfy California requirements

The ALTA standards do not displace state law. In California, the survey still has to be performed in a way that is consistent with California land surveying practice and any applicable state or local requirements. If the boundary work triggers a Record of Survey under California law, that is a separate issue that still has to be evaluated.

In other words, ALTA is not a shortcut around state practice. It is an additional national standard layered onto the survey.

The practical point

A good way to think about an ALTA survey is this: it is a boundary-and-title survey prepared for a transaction environment. It still depends on sound retracement and field evidence, but it goes further by organizing title items, access, improvements, easement evidence, and optional Table A information into a certified product that others can review.

That is why it shows up so often in commercial sales, refinancing, development due diligence, and other situations where the cost of being vague is higher than the cost of doing the survey carefully.

Common ALTA inputs

  • Current record description
  • Title commitment or other title evidence
  • Recorded easements and rights of way
  • Adjoiner information where needed
  • Selected Table A items

Common ALTA outputs

  • Boundary and monumentation
  • Buildings and visible improvements
  • Plottable easements and title notes
  • Access and right-of-way information
  • Required certification language

The scope often turns on title complexity, site conditions, and which optional Table A items are selected.
